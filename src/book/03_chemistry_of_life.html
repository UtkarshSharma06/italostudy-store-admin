BIOLOGY ● MOLECULAR FOUNDATIONS

01 — The Chemistry of Life

Introduction to Molecular Biology: Life is fundamentally a chemical phenomenon. Before we can understand how entire organisms breathe, run, or reproduce, we must first understand the microscopic building blocks that construct them. At the core, biology is driven by the interaction of carbon-based molecules floating in an aqueous environment. The structural forms these molecules take dictate their ultimate cellular functions—a concept known as the form-function relationship. In this chapter, we delve deeply into the unique properties of water, the elemental composition of biological matter, and the four major classes of biological macromolecules: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.

1.1 The Properties of Water

Water is the biological medium on Earth, and possibly the universe. All living organisms require water more than any other substance. Most cells are surrounded by water, and cells themselves are composed of 70 to 95 percent water. The remarkable properties of water arise from its unique molecular structure and the resultant intermolecular forces.

A water molecule (H₂O) consists of two hydrogen atoms bound to a highly electronegative oxygen atom in a distinct 'V' shape, with a bond angle of roughly 104.5 degrees. Because oxygen is far more electronegative than hydrogen, it pulls the shared electrons closer to its own nucleus. This unequal sharing of electrons makes water a polar molecule, meaning that its overall charge is unevenly distributed. The oxygen region carries a partial negative charge ($\delta^-$), while the hydrogen regions carry partial positive charges ($\delta^+$).

This polarity allows water molecules to form hydrogen bonds with each other. These hydrogen bonds are extraordinarily dynamic; they constantly break and reform at room temperature, a phenomenon that grants water its fluid nature. It is the cumulative power of billions of these weak hydrogen bonds that grants water its life-sustaining properties.

Cohesion, Adhesion, and Surface Tension

Cohesion refers to the phenomenon of water molecules sticking to one another via hydrogen bonding. This property is crucial in plants; it allows water to be pulled upward against gravity through narrow xylem vessels, from the roots to the highest leaves of colossal trees. Adhesion is the clinging of one substance to another. In plants, the adhesion of water to the cellular walls of the xylem helps counter the downward pull of gravity. Surface tension, a measure of how difficult it is to stretch or break the surface of a liquid, is exceptionally high in water due to the ordered arrangement of hydrogen bonds at the air-water interface.

Specific Heat and Insulation

Water has an incredibly high specific heat. It can absorb or release a massive amount of heat with only a slight change in its own temperature. This is because heat must first be absorbed to break hydrogen bonds before the subjective temperature (the kinetic energy of the molecules) can rise. This property buffers biological, terrestrial, and atmospheric temperatures, allowing life to persist within a stable thermal range. Furthermore, as water freezes, the hydrogen bonds lock the molecules into a crystalline lattice, separating them further than they are in a liquid state. Consequently, ice is less dense than liquid water and floats. If ice sank, all bodies of water would eventually freeze solid, making macroscopic life impossible.

Worked Example 1.1

Systematic Application: A CEnT-S question might ask why living systems utilize sweating as a cooling mechanism. The physiological reality is tied directly to the high heat of vaporization of water. Because hydrogen bonds must be broken for water to transition from a liquid to a gas, the departing water molecules carry away a significant amount of kinetic energy from the skin, resulting in evaporative cooling. Always link macro-phenomena (sweating) to micro-chemical properties (hydrogen bonding).

1.2 The Significance of Carbon

Carbon is unparalleled in its ability to form large, complex, and diverse molecules. A carbon atom possesses four valence electrons, which means it can form four robust covalent bonds with a variety of atoms. This tetravalence makes carbon uniquely suited as the structural backbone of life.

Carbon atoms can partner with each other to form long straight chains, highly branched chains, and complex ring structures. These carbon skeletons can include double and even triple bonds. The variation in carbon skeletons provides immense molecular diversity. Furthermore, functional groups (such as hydroxyl, carboxyl, amino, and phosphate groups) can be attached to the carbon skeleton, drastically changing the chemical reactivity and properties of the resulting molecule.

1.3 Macromolecules: An Overview

The vast majority of cellular machinery is constructed from giant molecules called macromolecules. These are usually polymers: long, complex chains consisting of many smaller, repeating building blocks identical or similar to one another, known as monomers. The synthesis and breakdown of these polymers follow a universal cellular logic.

Cells construct polymers via dehydration synthesis (or condensation reactions). In this process, two monomers are covalently bonded to each other with the loss of a water molecule. Conversely, polymers are broken down to monomers by hydrolysis, a process that is essentially the reverse of the dehydration reaction, where the insertion of a water molecule breaks the covalent bond. These processes are facilitated by enzymes—specialized macromolecular catalysts that speed up chemical reactions.

Tactical Warning

Understand the difference between a polymer and a macromolecule. While all proteins and nucleic acids are true polymers made of distinct monomers, Lipids are often termed macromolecules but are NOT true polymers, because they are not formed from a continuous repeating chain of a single type of simple monomer unit.

1.4 Carbohydrates: Fuel and Structure

Carbohydrates include both sugars and the polymers of sugars. Their primary function in the biological sphere involves rapid energy derivation and robust structural scaffolding.

The simplest carbohydrates are the monosaccharides, or simple sugars. Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) is the most common monosaccharide and is of central importance to the chemistry of life. Monosaccharides typically contain a carbonyl group (>C=O) and multiple hydroxyl groups (-OH). In aqueous solutions, most five- or six-carbon sugars form ring structures. When two monosaccharides are joined by a dehydration reaction, the resulting covalent bond is termed a glycosidic linkage. Important disaccharides include sucrose (table sugar, derived from glucose and fructose) and lactose (the sugar found in milk, derived from glucose and galactose).

Polysaccharides

Polysaccharides are macromolecules composed of hundreds to thousands of monosaccharides joined by glycosidic linkages. They serve as storage material or building structures.

  • Starch: A polymer of glucose monomers used by plants to stockpile surplus energy.
  • Glycogen: The animal equivalent of starch, heavily branched, and primarily stored in liver and muscle cells. It provides a rapid reserve of glucose during high-demand activities.
  • Cellulose: A major component of the tough walls that enclose plant cells. While it is a polymer of glucose, its glycosidic linkages differ geometrically from those in starch, resulting in straight, unbranched molecules that form rigid microfibrils. Most animals, including humans, lack the enzymes necessary to digest cellulose.

1.5 Lipids: Hydrophobic Powerhouses

Lipids are the one class of large biological molecules that does not include true polymers, and they are generally not big enough to be considered macromolecules. The unifying feature of lipids is that they mix poorly, if at all, with water. This hydrophobic behaviour is based on their molecular structure, which consists primarily of hydrocarbon regions with nonpolar covalent bonds.

Fats (Triglycerides)

A fat is constructed from two kinds of smaller molecules: glycerol and fatty acids. Glycerol is a three-carbon alcohol. A fatty acid has a long carbon skeleton, usually 16 or 18 carbon atoms in length, with a carboxyl group at one end. When three fatty acids undergo dehydration reactions to bind to a single glycerol molecule, they form a triacylglycerol (or triglyceride). The primary function of fats is dense, long-term energy storage. A gram of fat stores more than twice as much energy as a gram of a polysaccharide like starch.

Fats can be saturated or unsaturated. Saturated fatty acids have no double bonds between carbon atoms composing a chain, allowing them to pack tightly together and exist as solids at room temperature (like butter). Unsaturated fatty acids contain one or more double bonds—typically cis double bonds that create a "kink" in the hydrocarbon chain, preventing tight packing and keeping them liquid at room temperature (like olive oil).

Phospholipids

Phospholipids are absolutely essential for cells because they make up cell membranes. Their structure yields a classic example of how form fits function. A phospholipid is similar to a fat molecule, but it has only two fatty acids attached to glycerol rather than three. The third hydroxyl group of glycerol is joined to a negatively charged phosphate group. This dictates an amphipathic structure: the two hydrocarbon tails are intensely hydrophobic, retreating from water, while the phosphate "head" is hydrophilic. When exposed to an aqueous environment, phospholipids spontaneously self-assemble into a double-layered sheet called a bilayer, shielding their hydrophobic tails from water. This forms the foundational barrier of the cell.

Steroids

Steroids are lipids characterized by a carbon skeleton consisting of four fused rings. Different steroids are distinguished by the particular chemical groups attached to this ensemble of rings. Cholesterol is a crucial steroid in animals; it is a common component of animal cell membranes and is also the precursor from which other steroids, such as the sex hormones vertebrate testosterone and estrogen, are synthesized.

1.6 Proteins: The Machinery of Life

Proteins are the most structurally complex molecules known. They account for more than 50% of the dry mass of most cells and are instrumental in almost everything organisms do. Depending on their structure, proteins act as evolutionary catalysts (enzymes), structural support, storage, transport vehicles, cellular communicators, movement facilitators, and immune defense mechanisms.

Despite their diverse functions and extraordinary structural variations, all proteins are unbranched polymers constructed from the same core set of 20 distinct amino acids. A polymer of amino acids is called a polypeptide. A functional protein is not merely a polypeptide chain, but one or more polypeptides intricately twisted, folded, and coiled into a molecule of unique shape.

Amino Acid Structure

An amino acid is an organic molecule possessing both an amino group (-NH₂) and a carboxyl group (-COOH). At the center of the amino acid is an asymmetric carbon atom called the alpha ($\alpha$) carbon. Its four different partners are an amino group, a carboxyl group, a single hydrogen atom, and a variable group symbolized by R. The R group, also called the side chain, differs with each amino acid. The physical and chemical properties of the side chain (whether it is nonpolar and hydrophobic, polar and hydrophilic, acidic, or basic) determine the unique characteristics of a particular amino acid, thus dictating the final 3D structure of the protein.

When two amino acids are positioned so that the carboxyl group of one is adjacent to the amino group of the other, they can become joined by a dehydration reaction, resulting in a covalent bond called a peptide bond. Repeated over and over, this process yields a polypeptide.

The Four Levels of Protein Structure

A protein’s specific structure determines its exact function. This architecture is understood in four distinct tiers of complexity:

  1. Primary Structure: This is simply the linear, unbranched sequence of amino acids, dictated entirely by inherited genetic information. A slight change in primary structure—even a single amino acid substitution, as seen in sickle-cell disease—can permanently ruin a protein's function.
  2. Secondary Structure: The polypeptide chain repeatedly coils or folds in specific patterns that contribute to the protein's overall shape. This is a result of hydrogen bonds between the repeating constituents of the polypeptide backbone (not the side chains). Common structures include the $\alpha$-helix (a delicate coil) and the $\beta$-pleated sheet.
  3. Tertiary Structure: This is the overall, three-dimensional shape of a single polypeptide, resulting from interactions among the distinct R groups of the amino acids. These interactions encompass hydrophobic interactions, ionic bonds, extensive hydrogen bonds, and covalent disulfide bridges (which act as dense structural rivets).
  4. Quaternary Structure: Some macro-proteins consist of two or more polypeptide chains aggregated into one massive functional complex. Hemoglobin, the oxygen-carrying protein in red blood cells, is a textbook example, consisting of four distinct polypeptide subunits.
Denaturation

Protein structure depends on the physical and chemical conditions of the environment. If the pH, salt concentration, temperature, or other aspects of its environment are severely altered, the weak chemical bonds and interactions within a protein may be destroyed. This unravels the protein and causes it to lose its native shape, a process called denaturation. Because it is misshapen, the denatured protein is biologically inactive.

1.7 Nucleic Acids: The Blueprints

If primary protein structure ultimately determines cellular function, what determines primary protein structure? The amino acid sequence of a polypeptide is programmed by a discrete unit of inheritance known as a gene. Genes consist of DNA, which belongs to the class of compounds termed nucleic acids. Nucleic acids are polymers made of monomers called nucleotides.

There are two types of nucleic acids: Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and Ribonucleic acid (RNA). They enable living organisms to reproduce their complex components from one generation to the next. DNA provides the master directions for its own replication. It also directs RNA synthesis, and, through RNA, controls protein synthesis; this entire process is called gene expression.

Nucleotide Architecture

A nucleotide, in general, is composed of three parts: a nitrogen-containing base, a five-carbon sugar (a pentose), and one to three phosphate groups. In a polymer, each monomer has only one phosphate group. The nitrogenous bases fall into two families:

  • Pyrimidines: Possess one six-membered ring of carbon and nitrogen atoms. The members are cytosine (C), thymine (T, found only in DNA), and uracil (U, found only in RNA).
  • Purines: Are larger, characterized by a six-membered ring fused to a five-membered ring. The purines are adenine (A) and guanine (G).

The sugar is deoxyribose in DNA (which lacks an oxygen atom on the second carbon) and ribose in RNA.

Structure of DNA and RNA

DNA molecules have two polynucleotides that spiral around an imaginary axis, forming a highly stable double helix. The two sugar-phosphate backbones run in opposite 5' $\to$ 3' directions from each other, an arrangement referred to as antiparallel. The nitrogenous bases pair tightly in the interior of the helix via hydrogen bonding. Adenine always pairs with Thymine (forming two hydrogen bonds), and Guanine always pairs with Cytosine (forming three hydrogen bonds). This strict complementarity ensures exact replication of the genetic material.

Conversely, RNA molecules predominantly exist as single strands. Complementary base pairing can still occur, either between two RNA molecules or between parts of the same molecule, allowing RNA to take on particular three-dimensional shapes necessary for its function (such as transfer RNA).

Worked Example 1.2

Applying Chargaff's Rules: A viral DNA sample contains 22% Adenine. What is the percentage of Guanine in the genome?

Solution: Based on strictly complementary pairing, if A = 22%, then T = 22%. Together, A and T account for 44% of the genome. The remaining 56% must consist of G and C equally. Thus, dividing 56% by 2 reveals that Guanine comprises exactly 28% of the genome.
