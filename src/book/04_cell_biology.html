BIOLOGY ● CELL BIOLOGY & ORGANELLES

02 — Cell Biology and Organelles

Introduction to Cell Biology: If the molecules discussed in the previous section are the bricks, mortar, and wooden beams of life, then the cell is the fully constructed, functioning house. The cell is the fundamental unit of life; it is the lowest level of structure capable of performing all activities required for life. In the vast majority of CEnT-S exams, cell biology questions form the numerical majority. The section tests your understanding of exactly what components make up eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells, how these microscopic organelles divide labor, and how the cell manages to selectively let chemicals across its boundaries through passive and active transport mechanisms. Mastery of this spatial topography is non-negotiable.

2.1 The Fundamental Units of Life

In 1838, Matthias Schleiden and Theodor Schwann originally formulated what became known as the Cell Theory. Over decades of observation, the theory crystallized into three central tenets: (1) All living organisms are composed of one or more cells. (2) The cell is the basic unit of structure and organization in organisms. (3) Cells arise solely from pre-existing cells. Every breath you take, every thought you formulate, is the cumulative result of millions of cellular actions.

Despite their astonishing diversity, all cells share certain basic features. They are all bounded by a selective barrier, called the plasma membrane. Inside all cells is a semifluid, jellylike substance called cytosol, in which subcellular components are suspended. All cells contain chromosomes, which carry genes in the form of DNA. And all cells contain ribosomes, tiny complexes that synthesize proteins according to instructions from the genes.

2.2 Prokaryotic vs. Eukaryotic Architecture

A major distinction among types of cells is whether their genetic material is enclosed by a membrane. This division sorts all known life into two vast categories: Prokaryotes and Eukaryotes.

Prokaryotic Cells

Organisms of the domains Bacteria and Archaea consist of prokaryotic cells. They are defined by the absence of a true nucleus. Instead, the DNA is concentrated in a region that is not membrane-enclosed, called the nucleoid. Prokaryotes lack membrane-bounded organelles; they do not have mitochondria, chloroplasts, or an elaborate endoplasmic reticulum. They are generally much smaller than eukaryotic cells, typically 1–5 $\mu$m in diameter, which allows them rapid metabolic rates and extremely fast cellular division times.

Eukaryotic Cells

Protists, fungi, animals, and plants all consist of eukaryotic cells. The word eukaryote literally translates from Greek to mean "true kernel" or "true nucleus." In a eukaryotic cell, most of the DNA is housed in a distinct organelle bounded by a complex double membrane. Beyond the nucleus, the cytoplasm features an array of diverse, specialized membrane-bound organelles performing discrete functions. Eukaryotic cells are typically much larger than prokaryotes, ranging from 10 to 100 $\mu$m in diameter. This large size requires a dynamic network of internal membranes to divide the cell into compartments, heavily increasing the surface area available for cellular reactions.

Endosymbiotic Theory

An incredibly frequent exam topic is the evolutionary origin of eukaryotic complexity. The endosymbiotic theory states that an early ancestor of eukaryotic cells engulfed an oxygen-using, non-photosynthetic prokaryotic cell. Over billions of years, the engulfed cell evolved into mitochondria. A similarly engulfed photosynthetic prokaryote became the chloroplast. This is supported by the fact that mitochondria and chloroplasts contain their own circular DNA, possess 70S ribosomes (similar to bacteria), and replicate independently of the host cell via an asexual splitting process reminiscent of binary fission.

2.3 The Eukaryotic Endomembrane System

The endomembrane system includes a variety of organelles that are related either through direct physical continuity or by the transfer of membrane segments via tiny structural transport vesicles. It is the manufacturing and logistics backbone of the cell.

The Nucleus

The nucleus is the command center, containing most of the genes in the eukaryotic cell. It is enclosed by the nuclear envelope, an incredibly strict double membrane separating its contents from the cytoplasm. The envelope is perforated by pore complexes that highly regulate the entry and exit of proteins, RNA, and macromolecules. Within the nucleus, DNA is organized into discrete units called chromosomes. When a cell is not dividing, the complex of DNA and structural proteins (histones) exists in a diffuse, sprawling mass called chromatin. A prominent structure within the non-dividing nucleus is the nucleolus, the extremely dense site where ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is synthesized and the subunits of ribosomes are assembled.

The Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

The ER is an extensive network of membranous tubules and sacs called cisternae. The ER membrane is continuous with the nuclear envelope. There are two distinct, though connected, regions of the ER that differ in structure and function: rough ER and smooth ER.

  • Rough ER: So named because it is studded with millions of ribosomes on its outer surface. These ribosomes synthesize proteins that are destined to be secreted by the cell (like insulin) or embedded deeply into cellular membranes. As the polypeptide chain grows, it is threaded into the ER lumen, where it folds into its functional shape.
  • Smooth ER: Lacks ribosomes completely. Its enzymes function in diverse metabolic processes, which vary with cell type. These processes include the synthesis of lipids (oils, steroids, and new membrane phospholipids), the metabolism of carbohydrates, and the massive detoxification of drugs and poisons, particularly in liver cells. Additionally, it stores calcium ions used for muscle contraction signaling.

The Golgi Apparatus

After leaving the ER, many transport vesicles travel to the Golgi apparatus. Think of the Golgi as a cellular warehouse for receiving, sorting, modifying, and shipping. Here, the products of the ER, such as functional proteins, are modified and stored and then sent to other destinations. It consists of a stack of flattened membranous sacs (cisternae), and acts with a distinct structural polarity: the *cis* face receives vesicles from the ER, while the *trans* face pinches off vesicles destined for the plasma membrane or other sites.

Lysosomes

A lysosome is a membranous sac of hydrolytic enzymes that many eukaryotic cells use to digest macromolecules. Lysosomal enzymes work best in the acidic environment found deep within the lysosome. If a lysosome breaks open, the released enzymes are not very active because the outer cytosol has a near-neutral pH. However, massive leakage from many lysosomes can destroy a cell by autodigestion, causing necrosis. Lysosomes play critical roles in phagocytosis (eating smaller organisms or food particles) and autophagy (recycling the cell's own dead or damaged organelles).

2.4 Energy-Converting Organelles

Organisms must continuously extract energy from their surroundings and convert it into a usable cellular currency, primarily ATP. Two organelles act as the central turbines for this process.

Mitochondria

Mitochondria are the sites of cellular respiration, the metabolic process that uses oxygen to drive the generation of ATP by extracting energy from sugars, fats, and other fuels. They are enclosed by two membranes. The outer membrane is smooth, but the inner membrane is convoluted with extensive infoldings called cristae. These cristae give the inner mitochondrial membrane an astronomically large surface area, drastically enhancing the productivity of the proteins embedded within it that function in cellular respiration. The interior compartment enclosed by the inner membrane is called the mitochondrial matrix, housing the enzymes for the Krebs cycle as well as the mitochondrial DNA and ribosomes.

Chloroplasts

Found primarily in plants and algae, chloroplasts are the sites of photosynthesis. They convert immense solar energy to chemical energy by absorbing sunlight and using it to drive the synthesis of organic compounds such as sugars from carbon dioxide and water. The contents of a chloroplast are partitioned by an internal system of membranous sacs called thylakoids, which are sometimes stacked like poker chips. The fluid outside the thylakoids is the stroma, containing chloroplast DNA, ribosomes, and the enzymes of the Calvin cycle.

Worked Example 2.1

Applying Organelle Specialization: Based on organelle function, which cell type would have an overwhelming abundance of smooth endoplasmic reticulum?
A) Red blood cell
B) Liver hepatocyte
C) Skeletal muscle fiber
D) Pancreatic exocrine cell

Solution: The smooth ER's primary functions include lipid synthesis and drug detoxification. Liver hepatocytes are the body's primary detoxification centers, neutralizing alcohol and harmful metabolites, making option B the correct answer. The pancreatic cell (D) would instead be packed with rough ER for protein enzyme synthesis.

2.5 The Cytoskeleton

The cytoplasm is not a static slop; it is highly structured. The cytoskeleton is a network of fibers extending throughout the cytoplasm. It plays a major role in organizing the structures and activities of the cell and granting it mechanical support.

  • Microtubules: The thickest of the three types, they are hollow tubes made of tubulin dimers. They shape and support the cell and serve as tracks along which organelles equipped with motor proteins can move. They also drive the separation of chromosomes during cell division (mitosis).
  • Microfilaments (Actin Filaments): The thinnest, they are solid rods built from a twisted double chain of actin subunits. They are well known for their role in muscle contraction, interacting with myosin proteins.
  • Intermediate Filaments: Bear tension, reinforcing the structure of the cell. They are more permanent fixtures than the other two components, which are constantly disassembled and reassembled.

2.6 Cell Membranes and Membrane Transport

The plasma membrane is the edge of life. It exhibits selective permeability, allowing some substances to cross it more easily than others. The accepted paradigm for membrane structure is the fluid mosaic model: the membrane is a mosaic of protein molecules bobbing in a fluid bilayer of phospholipids.

Membrane proteins are broadly classed as integral (penetrating the hydrophobic core) or peripheral (loosely bound to the surface). These proteins act as transport channels, enzymatic activity sites, signal transducers, and markers for cell-cell recognition.

Passive Transport: Diffusion and Osmosis

Molecules possess inherent thermal kinetic energy, causing them to spread out evenly into available space. This unguided spreading is diffusion. In the absence of other forces, a substance will naturally diffuse from where it is more concentrated to where it is less concentrated—it will move down its concentration gradient. When this happens across a biological membrane without the cell expending energy, it is passive transport.

The diffusion of free water across a selectively permeable membrane is uniquely called osmosis. Water moves from an area of lower solute concentration (higher free water) to an area of higher solute concentration (lower free water). This concept governs cell survival:

  • Isotonic Solution: Solute concentration is the same inside and outside the cell. Water flows across the membrane equally in both directions. Animal cells do best here.
  • Hypertonic Solution: The surrounding solution has a higher solute concentration. The cell loses water, shrivels, and potentially dies.
  • Hypotonic Solution: The surrounding solution has a lower solute concentration. Water aggressively enters the cell. For an animal cell lacking a rigid wall, this risks lysis (bursting). A plant cell, equipped with a tough cellulose wall, becomes turgid (firm), which is theoretically healthy state for most plants.

Active Transport

To pump a solute across a membrane against its gradient requires work; the cell must expend energy, typically utilizing massive quantities of ATP. This is active transport. A paradigm example is the sodium-potassium pump, which continually exchanges Na$^+$ for K$^+$ across the plasma membrane of animal cells to maintain steep concentration gradients required for nerve impulses.

Bulk Transport

While small molecules cross via diffusion or protein pumps, large molecules cross in bulk, packaged in vesicles. Exocytosis secretes cellular products (like insulin) when a transport vesicle fuses with the plasma membrane. Endocytosis is the reverse: the cell takes in biological molecules by pinching off new vesicles from the plasma membrane, acting as a microscopic ingestion mechanism.
