BIOLOGY ● BIOENERGETICS

03 — Bioenergetics and Metabolism

Introduction to Bioenergetics: A living cell is a miniature chemical factory where thousands of reactions occur within a microscopic space. Sugars are converted to amino acids, amino acids to proteins, and proteins are broken down for fuel. All of these require a strict management of energy resources. Bioenergetics is the study of how energy flows through living organisms. The CEnT-S syllabus demands granular knowledge of two monumental biological pathways: Cellular Respiration (the chaotic breakdown of glucose to yield ATP) and Photosynthesis (the pristine synthesis of glucose utilizing quantum packets of light). If you understand the flow of electrons through these systems, you conquer one of the hardest segments of the test.

3.1 Metabolism and Thermodynamics

Metabolism is the totality of an organism's chemical reactions. It manages the material and energy resources of the cell in highly coordinated, multi-step metabolic pathways. These pathways are generally classified into two opposing forces:

  • Catabolic Pathways: Release energy by breaking down complex molecules into simpler compounds. A major pathway of catabolism is cellular respiration, in which sugar glucose and other organic fuels are broken down in the presence of oxygen to carbon dioxide and water.
  • Anabolic Pathways: Consume energy to build complex molecules from simpler ones; they are sometimes called biosynthetic pathways. An example is the synthesis of an amino acid from simpler molecules and the synthesis of a protein from amino acids.

All metabolism is subject to the undeniable laws of thermodynamics. The First Law asserts that the energy of the universe is constant; it can be transferred and transformed, but it cannot be created or destroyed. The Second Law asserts that every energy transfer or transformation intimately increases the entropy (disorder) of the universe. Consequently, cells cannot simply recycle a closed loop of energy; they must constantly extract highly ordered energy (like food or sunlight) from the environment and dispel disorganized energy (heat).

3.2 ATP: The Cellular Currency

A cell performs three main kinds of heavy labor: chemical work (pushing endergonic reactions), transport work (pumping substances against the direction of spontaneous movement), and mechanical work (the beating of cilia or the contraction of muscle cells). Almost without exception, the immediate source of energy that powers cellular work is Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP).

ATP contains the sugar ribose, with the nitrogenous base adenine and a chain of three phosphate groups attached to it. The bonds between the phosphate groups can be violently broken by hydrolysis. When the terminal phosphate bond is broken, a molecule of inorganic phosphate (P_i) leaves the ATP, which becomes Adenosine Diphosphate (ADP), and significant free energy is released. The cell utilizes this release by chemically coupling the broken phosphate group to another molecule, driving an otherwise uphill reaction.

3.3 Cellular Respiration: The Harvesting of Electrons

How do cells regenerate their continuous need for ATP? By tapping into the energy stored in food. Cellular respiration is a brutal, controlled combustion. The overall chemical equation for the aerobic respiration of glucose is:

$C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2 \rightarrow 6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{Energy (ATP + heat)}$

This process is fundamentally a redox (reduction-oxidation) reaction. The fuel (glucose) is oxidized, yielding its electrons, while oxygen is ultimately reduced, violently taking those electrons to form massive stability in the form of water. The energy drawn down from these falling electrons powers the regeneration of ATP. Respiration unfolds in a cascading series of three distinct stages.

The Critical Role of NAD$^+$

Electrons are not handed directly from glucose to oxygen; that would cause an explosive release of heat unable to be harnessed by the cell. Electrons are first passed to electron carrier coenzymes, most prominently NAD$^+$. When NAD$^+$ accepts two electrons and one proton, it is reduced to NADH. NADH represents highly stored energy that will be aggressively tapped to make ATP later in the process.

Stage 1: Glycolysis

Glycolysis literally means "sugar splitting" and occurs universally in the cytosol of almost every cell on earth, an ancient evolutionary relic that requires absolutely no oxygen. A single six-carbon glucose molecule is broken down into two molecules of a three-carbon compound called pyruvate.

Glycolysis is divided into an energy investment phase and an energy payoff phase. The cell actually has to spend 2 ATP to destabilize the robust glucose ring. However, the payoff yields 4 ATP and 2 NADH per glucose. Therefore, the net energy yield from glycolysis per glucose molecule is 2 ATP plus 2 NADH.

Stage 2: Pyruvate Oxidation and the Citric Acid Cycle

If molecular oxygen (O$_2$) is present, the pyruvate aggressively enters a mitochondrion. Before the cycle can begin, pyruvate undergoes a highly transitional step where it is converted into a two-carbon compound called acetyl coenzyme A (acetyl CoA), releasing the first molecule of carbon dioxide (CO$_2$) and generating another NADH per pyruvate.

The acetyl CoA then feeds its monumental acetyl group into the Citric Acid Cycle (also known as the Krebs cycle), which takes place in the fluid mitochondrial matrix. The cycle functions as a metabolic furnace that strictly tears apart the final remnants of the glucose molecule. For every turn of the cycle (which spins twice for every molecule of glucose, once for each remaining pyruvate), it severely strips away electrons to form 3 NADH and 1 FADH$_2$, generates 1 molecule of ATP, and releases 2 molecules of CO$_2$ as pure exhaust waste. Following the completion of the Krebs cycle, the original glucose molecule is entirely eradicated, transformed fully into six CO$_2$ molecules.

Stage 3: Oxidative Phosphorylation

Up to this critical point, only a pathetic 4 net ATP have been generated. The vast majority of the extracted energy is locked within the electron carriers: 10 NADH and 2 FADH$_2$. In the final stage, taking place across the intricately folded inner mitochondrial membrane, the cell cashes in.

The electron transport chain (ETC) is a heavily sequenced collection of multiprotein complexes embedded in the inner membrane. The electron carriers drop off their high-energy electrons at the "top" of the chain. The electrons cascade down the chain from one carrier protein to the next in a series of downhill redox reactions. At the very bottom, molecular oxygen (the most highly electronegative component in the entire system) serves as the final electron acceptor, picking up a pair of electrons, grabbing two ambient protons, and permanently reducing into stable water.

The monumental, overarching purpose of the ETC is not immediately to make ATP. As proteins pass the electrons downhill through the membrane, the massive burst of energy is utilized by the protein complexes to pump protons (H$^+$) from the mitochondrial matrix outward into the intermembrane space. This creates an unbelievably dense hydrogen ion gradient—a proton-motive force, a battery built across a membrane.

Chemiosmosis: The protons can only flow back down into the matrix by passing through a highly complex rotary enzyme embedded in the membrane called ATP synthase. The intense flow of protons through this channel physically spins a rotor on the enzyme, utilizing mechanical kinetic energy to ram microscopic quantities of ADP and free phosphate together to synthesize massive quantities of ATP. This final stage yields approximately 26 to 28 ATP molecules per original glucose.

Worked Example 3.1

Identifying Poison Mechanics: Cyanide is a lethal poison deeply referenced in admissions tests. It works by blocking the final complex in the electron transport chain (Cytochrome C Oxidase). Why is this fatal within minutes?

Solution: By blocking the final complex, cyanide prevents electrons from being passed downstream to oxygen. The entire electron transport chain essentially creates a deadly "traffic jam." NADH and FADH$_2$ can no longer drop off their electrons. No electrons flowing means no protons are pumped. The gradient collapses. ATP synthase ceases spinning. The cell runs completely out of ATP and instantly dies of energy starvation.

3.4 Fermentation: Anaerobic Survival

Without the highly electronegative oxygen to ruthlessly pull electrons down the transport chain, oxidative phosphorylation instantly ceases. How do cells survive? They fall back extensively on glycolysis, which requires no oxygen and nets 2 ATP per glucose. But there is a terminal problem: glycolysis requires a steady supply of NAD$^+$ to accept electrons. If all NAD$^+$ is heavily reduced to NADH, and the ETC is unable to empty it, glycolysis will paralyze.

Fermentation is simply a survival extension of glycolysis that allows continuous generation of ATP by explicitly providing a mechanism to rapidly recycle NAD$^+$.

  • Alcohol Fermentation: Pyruvate is converted to ethanol (ethyl alcohol) in two steps. This occurs in yeast, regenerating NAD$^+$ and releasing massive quantities of CO$_2$ (which causes bread to rise).
  • Lactic Acid Fermentation: Pyruvate is reduced directly by NADH to form lactate as an end product, with absolutely no release of CO$_2$. Human muscle cells make massive use of lactic acid fermentation to wildly generate ATP when oxygen is desperately scarce during strenuous exercise.

3.5 Photosynthesis: Building Glucose from Light

Respiration tears down organic fuel. Photosynthesis builds it out of nothing but thin air, groundwater, and violently intercepted star rays. It is precisely the reverse of respiration.

$6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{Light energy} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$

The process occurs in the chloroplasts of plants and algae and is conceptually divided into two monumental stages: the Light-Dependent Reactions and the Calvin Cycle.

The Light Reactions (The Photo Part)

Occurring physically in the flattened thylakoid membranes within the chloroplast, these reactions fundamentally convert intense solar energy to potent chemical energy. Embedded within the thylakoid membrane are Photosystems (dense clusters of light-harvesting chlorophyll pigments and proteins). When a massive photon of light strikes a chlorophyll molecule, it violently excites an electron to a higher energy state. This volatile electron is immediately captured by a primary electron acceptor.

To replace this lost electron, an incredibly powerful enzyme system viciously splits a water molecule (H$_2$O) apart into protons, electrons, and highly reactive oxygen. This specific action is the sole origin of all the breathable oxygen in the Earth's atmosphere.

The captured energized electrons travel down a dense electron transport chain. Just like in heavily respiring mitochondria, their fall forces protons across the membrane, establishing a steep gradient. Protons rushing endlessly back through ATP synthase rigorously generate ATP. Furthermore, the exhausted electrons receive an enormous secondary photon boost at Photosystem I, allowing them to finally be handed over to a critical electron carrier, NADP$^+$, thereby reducing it to fiercely reactive NADPH. Thus, the light reactions produce exactly two critical products for cellular use: ATP (energy) and NADPH (reducing powder).

The Calvin Cycle (The Synthesis Part)

The Calvin cycle operates entirely in the dense stroma (the fluid surrounding the thylakoids). It is totally independent of light directly, running solely off the massive energy budget supplied by the light reactions.

The cycle incorporates gaseous CO$_2$ directly from the air into organic molecules already present in the chloroplast. This is biological carbon fixation. The cycle aggressively utilizes the chemical energy of ATP and the violent reducing power of electrons carried by NADPH to convert the CO$_2$ to a strict, usable three-carbon carbohydrate (G3P). The cycle must turn three times, fixing three separate molecules of CO$_2$, to yield one singular molecule of G3P that can cleanly exit the cycle. Two molecules of G3P can be highly linked together in standard metabolic tracks to forge a single molecule of glucose.
